The first competition of my life

发表于 2021-08-31

An event which sparked a period of personal growth and a new understanding of myself was my first participation in the musical instrument competition held by my primary school.

I used to be a shy girl. Although I enjoyed playing a kind of traditional chinese musical instrument called the guzheng, I was not brave enough to play it in front of the public. As a result, almost no one knew that I could play the guzheng apart from my relatives and teachers.

However, this situation changed when I was in the third grade. My music teacher thought that I should be willing to take challenges to show everyone my unique skills and be more confident, so she recommended me to the school to take part in the instrument competition. I wanted to refuse, but my parents also encouraged me to take this rare opportunity. Finally, I made up my mind to prepare for this competition seriously as I did not want to let down the people in my life who have always supported me. During the preparation for this competition, I was still very nervous and anxious. However, my parents told me that the meaning and purpose of the competition was not only to win prizes, but also to help me become a better person through this kind of experience. From then on, I started to enjoy practicing playing the guzheng and felt happy because both my ability and my confidence improved. After several months of hard preparation, I won the second prize. I found I was able to achieve the success which I had wanted.

Through my experience of this event, I changed from being timid and introverted to being brave, confident and able to cherish any opportunities in my life. The music I played at the ten-year celebration of junior school was appreciated by my classmates and teachers. I am now a member of the high school symphonic band. I also take an active part in other club activities or subject competitions. I really enjoy the feeling of self-improvement.

Personal goal

发表于 2021-07-31

I set a personal goal for myself which was to pass IELTS with an overall band score of 6.5 as I will apply for university with this score

The preparation took over 4 months of hard work involved doing past papers and so on. With the help of my teachers, I completed and corrected all the daily tasks according to the plan made by my class advisor. As I also had a lot of homework to do from my school at the same time, I found that I was not used to such a fast-paced lifestyle. However, I soon got used to the workload as my teachers gave me guidance such as helping me find a balance between my school curriculum, IELTS study and activities held by school clubs. I started to enjoy a more well-organized life which gave me the experience of how to be focused, organized and then work more efficiently.

I felt very depressed for a period of time at first. Because I was studying a lot, yet made little progress. Then my teachers suggested a different way of collating my mistakes to make it easier to learn from them and this suggestion did help me a lot. Due to this, I found my weakness was not organized so I followed my teachers to overcome this.

As the old saying goes, ‘No pain, no gain.’ I achieved this goal a few days ago. Now, I am very grateful to my teachers and myself as I did not give up because of their encouragement. I realize that there is a strong possibility to achieve your personal goal with hard work. Being organized have improves the efficiency of my studying. As a result, I have more free time to pursue my own personal interests.

Draft Resolution

发表于 2021-07-31

Committee: General Assembly Third Committee

Topic: Gender Equality in COVID Pandemic

Sponsors: Islamic Republic of Iran, Japan, People’s Republic of China, Singapore, The Kingdom of Saudi Arabia The Republic of India, The people’s Democratic Republic of Algeria

Signatories: The Kingdom of the Netherlands, Ukraine

​

The United Nations General Assembly Third Committee,

Recalling UN resolution A/RES/52/86,A/RES/52/93, A/RES/74/126, A/74/222, R/48/104 DEVPA, A/RES/74/128,A/RES/74/127,

Recognizing the existence of gender inequality and its negative impact on women’s rights globally,

Aware of the deterioration of gender inequality during the COVID-19 pandemic,

Appealing for specific solutions to gender inequality issues in all fields,

Affirming the majority of health workers on the front-line are women,

Recognizing among those respondents in employment when the pandemic struck, 5 percent of the women are now unemployed, compared with just 2.8 of the men,

Noticing that the COVID-19 pandemic has accelerated automation and digitization, leading to poor reemployment prospects for women,

Recognizing the rise of domestic violence in COVID pandemic,

Emphasizing the importance of solving gender inequality issues,

Reaffirming the necessity to implement suitable policies depending on the current situation of each country,

Part I Gender equality situation during the COVID-19 pandemic

  1. Realizes that women’s rights are still violated, and that the situation of gender inequality is becoming severer during the COVID-19 pandemic including the aspects below:

(1) Health

(2) Unemployment, inequality in working opportunities and income

(3) Unpaid work

(4) Gender-based violence

(5) Politics

(6) Stereotypes towards women

(7) Influence of religion

2.Notes the diversity of inequality situation in each country;

3.Encourages each country to select the suitable policies to implement domestically;

4.Calls all countries to pay attention to the inequality issue and give corresponding and specific solutions;

PART II Health gender inequality in COVID Pandemic

5.Draws attention to the lack of medical and daily necessities for front-line women health workers;

6. Calls upon governments to provide vaccination first and sufficient medical and daily necessities including protective suit, medical masks, alcohol-based hand sanitizer and so on for front-line health workers, especially considering needs of female health workers like tampons;

7.Recommends member states to ensure the provision, through the primary healthcare system, of universal access of couples and individuals to appropriate and affordable preventive services with respect to COVID pandemic;

8.Further recommends member states to expand the provision of counselling and voluntary and confidential diagnostic and treatment services for women;

9.Draws attention to the promotion of mutually respectful and equitable relations between women and men concerning health service needs under COVID Pandemic;

10.Suggests providing more accessible, available and affordable primary healthcare services of high quality including sexual, reproductive, vaccination healthcare through significant increase in investment in social and medical infrastructure;

11.Suggests facilitating coordination and cooperation among governments, departments, agencies, offices and organizations to build a well-bedded system for promoting women’s healthy welfare under COVID pandemic;

12.Suggests increasing participation of women in medical research such as medical trials;

PART II Unemployment

13.Proposes the convening of another Global Leaders’ Meeting on gender equality and women’s empowerment in 2025 to contribute to women’s development in post-pandemic era;

14.Suggests implementing social and income protection mechanisms for alternative employment models to ensure appropriate coverage for all workers in the formal and informal economy, with special attention to essential workers, part-time workers and the self-employed;

15.Recommends to design and test innovative solutions such as portable benefits which workers could carry across multiple jobs;

16.Suggests providing vocational skill training, online courses equally for women and men during COVID pandemic;

17.Calls on governments to eliminate regulatory, legislative, and sociocultural barriers that prevent equal access of women to financial products and services;

18.Calls on governments to make good use of domestic financial institutions, such as banks, and encourage charity fund organizations or companies to provide financial assistance for women in urgent need and ensure women’s access to financial services and products;

19.Suggests adopting gender-responsive budgeting informed by gender impact assessment to ensure that post-pandemic recovery measures foster a gender-inclusive workforce;

20.Calls for investment in supporting infrastructure such as childcare, parental leave and education, in order to increase women’s confidence to reenter the labour market after pandemic;

21.Calls on governments to provide unemployment benefits for fired women, in order to:

(1) Help individuals who have lost their job to sustain a necessary consumption level;

(2) Help creating a more leisurely period of time, so that there will be a bigger possibility for unemployed women to find suitable and sustainable jobs and avoid entering an endless loop of unemployment;

(3) Encourage shifting the business of companies that have been hit severely by the pandemic to those that have been rapidly developing for the stimulation of COVID-19;

22.Suggests instructing domestic citizens to modify conventional stereotypes towards women;

23.Recommends countries to refer to suggestions below when introducing or expanding the use of gender quota, including but not limited to:

(1) The main goal is to ensure that women can overcome obstacles in entering male dominated industries;

(2) Conducting trials in administrative field to which gender quota has contributed more, in order to examine the effects of gender-quota on both positive and negative sides;

(3) Deciding domestic gender quota standard according to countries’ actual situation;

(4) Considering women’s will and how to make up for the novel gap caused by the transfer of women’s proportions labour force;

PART III Unpaid Work

24.Calls on governments to measure the true value of women’s unpaid care and domestic work and its estimated contribution to the economy;

25.Suggests increasing the unpaid work for males who can spare time on family work;

26.Strongly suggests raising the public awareness of gender imbalance in responsibility for domestic care and housework, especially under COVID pandemic;

27.Suggests engaging with men and masculinity as an integral part of securing a positive shift in norms and behaviours about unpaid work;

28.Suggests taking action to correct social norms that undermine women’s agency and access to opportunity for social and economic empowerment;

29.Suggests recognizing and valuing the hidden work that women are doing through gender-disaggregated data and gender-sensitive programming;

30.Suggests redistributing care work between households and the state in the form of government-provided care services for children, people with disabilities and the elderly;

PART IV Gender-based violence

31.Calls on countries to affirm the severity of gender-based violence since gender-based violence apparently threatens the physical health of women, as well as their psychological health, doubling the psychological burden of women caused by COVID pandemic;

32.Calls upon the resolution of ending violence by gender of all forms, including but not limited to raping, sexual harassment, etc.;

33.Draws attention to new forms of gender-based violence, cyber violence towards women for example;

34.Calls on countries to be aware of the different issues each country faces to resolve gender-based violence, including but not limited to cultures and religions;

35.Suggests creating or strengthening institutional mechanisms so that women and girls can report acts of violence against them in a safe and confidential environment, free from the fear of penalties or retaliation, and file charges;

36.Calls on countries to ensure that women with disability have access to information and services in the field of violence against women during and after COVID pandemic;

37.Recommends the methods below to reduce gender-based violence:

(1) Advocating education on gender equality and methods for boycotting gender-based violence;

(2) Establishing laws against gender-based violence, with specified definition of gender-based violence and the corresponding penalties to the perpetrators;

(3) Enhancing the existing mechanisms for women to combat gender-based violence;

(4) Providing hotline and shelter services for women who suffer from gender-based violence and need assistance and consultation;

(5) Collecting data on gender-based violence as a reference of implementation of corresponding policies;

(6) Opposing extremists’ misinterpretation and use of certain religious doctrines;

(7) Strengthening the online censorship of contents showing discrimination and aggression towards women.

Working Paper 4

发表于 2021-07-31

Sponsors: China, India, Iran, Japan,Singapore

Part I Effects of pandemic on women employment

i. Recognizing among those respondents in employment when the pandemic struck, 5 percent of the women are now unemployed, compared with just 2.8 of the men.

ii. Noticing that LinkedIn data shows that the number of women in leadership positions has dropped significantly, leading to a reversal of progress in multiple industries in one to two years.

iii. Noticing that the COVID-19 pandemic has accelerated automation and digitization, and intensified the damage to the labour market. Due to the gradual increase in “two shifts” and the combined effects of occupational segregation and other labour market dynamics, this epidemic is likely to have a traumatic impact on women’s future economy and may lead to poor reemployment prospects.

Part II Effects of pandemic on women income

i. Aware of that women have seen their earnings decline almost two-thirds more sharply than man, dropping by 16.5 percent on average since the pandemic began, compared with a drop of 10.1 percent for men.

ii. Noticing the situation as regards personal disposable income is almost identical at the global level. In every market surveyed, women report a steeper decline in the ability to pay for non-essential goods and services than men, ranging from 73% in South Africa to 19% in the UK.

iii. Realizing that the pandemic has shown that quarantine measures greatly weakened women’ s economic capacity and increased poverty rate and food insecurity of women living in poor regions.

iv. Further realizing that due to deep-rooted structural reasons, women’s income is 16% lower than men’s at global average level, and the wage gap in some countries can reach 35%.

Part III Measures to alleviate women unemployment and loss of income in pandemic

i. Implement social and income protection mechanisms for alternative employment models to ensure appropriate coverage for all workers in the formal and informal economy, with special attention to essential workers, part-time workers and the self employed.

ii. Design and test innovative solutions such as portable benefits which workers could carry across multiple jobs.

iii. Eliminate regulatory, legislative, and sociocultural barriers that prevent equal access of women to financial products and services.

iv. Call on governments to cooperate with UNICEF, UN Women, UNESCO, make good use of domestic financial institutions, such as banks, and encourage charity fund organizations or companies to provide financial assistance for women in urgent need and ensure women’s access to financial services and products.

v. Adopt gender-responsive budgeting informed by gender impact assessment to ensure that pandemic recovery measures foster a gender-inclusive workforce.

vi. Suggest instructing domestic citizens to modify conventional stereotypes towards women.

Part III Controversies on the gender quota

Noting the different attitudes towards the solution of gender quota;

i. Supportive of gender quota

Countries such as New Zealand is supportive of gender quota. The reason given is that gender quota contributes to the adjustments to the gender percentages in certain occupations, thus contributing to the process of gender equality in economics. To be more specifically, the supportive countries attach importance to the percentage of gender in employment, which is regarded by the supportive countries as a sign of gender equality in employment.

ii. Opposite to gender quota

Countries like Iran and Lichtenstein are opposite to gender quota. For instance, the reasons given by Iran are that the choices of employment by citizens, whether males or females, shouldn’t be interfered by mandatory standards of gender percentage of employment. Also, Iran reminds that gender quota is a mandatory standard controlling the gender percentage in employment intentionally, yet this can’t solve the fundamental issue of gender equality. For instance, the fundamental factor causing gender equality in Iran is conventional perspectives towards women. To ensure the successful implementation of gender equality, those stereotypes should be corrected first, or the execution of gender quota is just limited to balancing the percentages of gender in employment, rather than solve the problem in a sustainable way.

-Reminding that gender quota is not a mandatory method for the international society;

-Encouraging countries holding different views towards gender quota to choose the policy that suits the domestic situation of the nation, including but not limited to gender quota.

ii. To reach a consensus

China advocates countries which have not implement gender-quota but hope to attempt this policy can conduct trials in areas like administration to test the effects of gender-quota. And China suggests governments organizing domestic assessment experts on political effectiveness to judge if gender-quota is completely suitable. If not, governments could take other measures and improve current policies to promote women participation in decision-making.

Part IV Outlook

Outlook

Noticing the violence against women during the is a commonplace, we divide into three parts to carry out a profound discussion. The three parts are domestic violence and society violence, sexual violence, and the laws and suggestions respectively.

Domestic violence and society violence

The control of the epidemic requires people to avoid gathering so isolation have increased the possibility of domestic violence. With people confined to their homes under the pandemic, many more women reached out for help. One of the main reasons is that some women have no financial resources which lead to the shrinking family income. Economic problems have led to domestic violence, however, this problem has yet to be resolved, however, seeking help for “family matters” is still a taboo in Japanese society. As a result, we should take proactive measures such as establishing emergency shelters for those who have suffered domestic violence to help women escape from the shadow pandemic.

At the same time, because of the increase in the female unemployment rate, the financial burden of the family has also led to an increase in the suicide rate. This is social violence against women.

Sexual violence

From Indian perspective, sexual violence is a big problem.

There are many reasons for the Washington posted delves into the root cause of the sexual violence in India. Thirdly, the lack of public safety. Women generally lack of protection. Rape occurred on the bus, even the Indian authorities have said India is unsafe for women in public places. The fourth is not convicted. In India have reported the case of rape, only less than 26% of the cases have to convict prisoner. Last but not least, low status of women. For poor families, daughter’s dowry is a family burden greatly. Due to sex-selective abortions and female infanticide, India is the world’s one of the lowest populations of female to male ratio. In India, the son of the treatment is better than daughters, forever and easier to get the chance to receive education, so employment prospects are better.

law and advice

The Asia-Pacific region lags behind the world in the level of legal protection for women, as well as the frequency and intensity of government voices on gender inequality. This stems from the traditions and religions of the Asia-pacific region, which, as we have mentioned, cannot be changed in a short period of time. So we propose that the law be used to protect women’s rights more effectively.

1.For developing countries, legal policies in favour of women should be established. Such as health products tax exemption, marriage compensation regulations, etc.

Position Paper

发表于 2021-07-31

Delegate: Zhao Jie
Delegation: Japan
Committee: The Third Committee of the United Nations General Assembly
Topic: Gender equality in COVID pandemic
School: Jinling High School

Introduction

The patriarchal clan society led to gender discrimination as most men had an advantage over women in physical strength, however, gender inequality still exists these days. Since the spread of the COVID-19, some situations even have made it more serious. For example, many companies tend to fire female employees to ensure their profits. This has caused dissatisfaction among many people.
As the builders and contributors of society, women have the right to fight for and take back the opportunities they should have. Moreover, eliminating gender discrimination can improve social civilization, increase productivity, and promote the emergence of more talents. So it is important for us to help women and take actions to eliminate gender inequality.

Past actions

The United Nations passed The Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination against Women at the General Assembly on December 18, 1979, and it entered into force in September 1981. It is an international convention on the rights of women and promotes the feminist movement through international treaties. It is also an important international human rights instrument formulated by the United Nations to eliminate discrimination against women and strive for gender equality. The convention establishes rules to protect women’s rights in politics, law, work, education, medical services, business activities and family relations. UN Women claimed that many women’s industries were hit hard by the COVID-19. Women are increasingly facing the difficulty of making a living. There will be more than 47 million women and children living below the poverty line in the world. At the same time, UN Women called on all the countries to strengthen solidarity and cooperation to promote gender equality and women’s empowerment.
Since Japanese companies use the annual merit-based wage system for their wages, companies are tend to recruit male employees. In order to solve the serious gender discrimination of Japanese companies in recruiting employees, Japan has banned filling in gender and pasting photos on resumes.

Position of Japan

Due to the influence of Japanese traditional culture, there is still serious gender discrimination in Japanese society. The COVID-19 has made gender discrimination harming Japanese women’s lives more, especially those who living at the bottom of society. Gender inequality has no benefits to the development and civilization progress, so Japan supports the elimination of gender discrimination. As a developed country, Japan will actively take equal rights measures.
On December 18, the World Economic Forum (WEF) released the “2018 Global Gender Difference Report”. Among the 149 countries surveyed, Japan ranked 110th (with a total score of 0.662) and was still the last place in the G7 group. The main reason for Japan’s low ranking is its low scores in the economic and political sectors. Excessive income gap between men and women (103, score 0.527) and large gap in the number of men and women in management positions (129, score 0.152) have a greater impact. In the education and health care parts, Japan has achieved a high score of nearly full score.
According to this report, Japan will investigate if companies treat male and female employees differently and introduce policies to narrow the wage gap between male and female employees to ensure women’s income. At the same time, the government will stipulate the ratio of male and female employees in enterprises to solve the problem of high unemployment among women. Japan will also encourage women to participate in politics and improve women’s social status. At the same time, Japan will maintain equality in education and health. We will also fight against those who think that women should do housework to take care of children and the elderly for unpaid work in home. Develop social discussions on a larger scale and awaken people’s awareness to achieve gender equality is the result that beneficial to the public.
